Research Area

People excel at real-life tasks that remain challenging for modern AI — planning, generalizing from a few examples, and inventing new tools on the fly. How do we solve real-world problems so efficiently?

Our lab builds models of intelligence in humans and machines, grounding computational models in cognitive science, AI theory, and empirical experimentation.

The lab explores questions such as:
– How do people plan, navigate, and explore in the real world?
– How do people formulate world models and generate hypotheses about how the world works?
– How can humans and machines work together to invent new concepts and tools?

Working in our lab

Our lab connects a collaborative group of researchers at various career stages, with active international collaborations. We draw on methods across artificial intelligence, cognitive science, psychology, and neuroscience.

Lab workflow is organized around distinct projects with weekly meetings. Projects are scoped around conference deadlines, with an eventual journal publication, after which the project wraps up. Students are expected to gradually develop independence and lead projects of their own.

Conference participation is important for integrating into the international scientific community, and the lab prioritizes funding travel to major AI and cognitive science conferences. We also encourage submitting to smaller conferences with strong networking opportunities (e.g. COSYNE, CCN).

Graduate students are encouraged to actively seek scholarships, fellowships, and stack multiple funding sources — this is standard practice in the Canadian academic model. Advisor funding is available to lab members, but the Canadian funding structure is not designed around a single funding source as a living income. Funding acquisition is therefore treated as a technical skill that all graduate students should develop.

We accept expressions of interest from prospective PhD students and undergraduates interested to build research experience.

While I do not generally offer funding MSc students (ocasional support is available for Canadian applicants), MSc is not required for prospective PhD applicants with prior research experience.

Lab member responsibilities

  • Write code, develop, and implement computational models
  • Prepare weekly or bi-weekly slides and progress reports for meetings with academic advisors
  • Work effectively with the group toward conference deadlines (NeurIPS, ICLR, Cognitive Science Society)
  • Assist with, and eventually lead, journal paper preparation (PLOS, Cognition, Nature group journals)
  • Analyze data, fit statistical models, and generate plots
  • Design behavioral experiments and write scripts to process behavioral data
  • Present work as posters and talks, informally to collaborators and at conferences
  • Contribute to teaching at the master's and bachelor's level as a teaching assistant
  • Proactively apply for scholarships and seek funding opportunities

PhD candidate requirements

  • Strong mathematical foundations — ability or willingness to formulate and prove theorems and analyze algorithms theoretically. Strong psychology or neuroscience graduates without a solid math background, but a willingness to learn foundations, are still welcome to apply.
  • Successfully completed a Master's degree, or equivalent research experience, in a quantitative discipline: cognitive psychology, neuroscience, mathematics, physics, computer science, or related fields.
  • A record of publications at international venues, or prior research experience with strong references.
  • A working knowledge of programming languages (e.g. Python, R), statistics, and AI/ML.
  • Excellent written and spoken English.
  • Self-motivated, organized, and proactive.
  • Familiarity with LLM API, scientific computing libraries, and coding agents is an asset.

Join Us!

Contact us to express interest. Undergraduate applications for directed study and honours projects are welcome at any time.

Please include in your email:

  • Which research questions you want to work on
  • Which papers, books, or blog posts have shaped your thinking
  • Your background in mathematics, psychology, and prior research experience
  • Your CV

We do not respond to AI-generated emails, generic inquiries, or emails that do not address the above. We will try to respond to all promising applicants.

The easiest way to join as a PhD student is to have worked with us previously, by contributing to one of our papers. The easiest way to join as an undergraduate is to send your CV, transcript, and a short email outlining your background in mathematics, psychology, and prior research experience.
